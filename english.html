Sentence Structure

How clauses combine into simple, compound, complex, and compound-complex sentences — plus the conjunctions that hold them together.

Summary Concept cards Exam practice

Language hierarchy

Grammar nests inside itself, largest to smallest:

Sentences → Clauses → Phrases → Words

A sentence is built from one or more clauses. A clause is built from one or more phrases. A phrase is built from one or more words. Everything below comes back to that one idea.

What actually makes something a sentence?

A sentence needs at least one independent clause: a subject and a verb that together express a complete thought and can stand alone. A striking image, a single noun phrase, or a fragment of dialogue might communicate plenty — but without a subject and a verb of its own, it isn't a sentence in the grammatical sense.

Clauses can nest inside other clauses. Consider:
[ I think [ I'd like coffee. ] ]
[ He said [ I think [ I would like coffee. ] ] ]

The first has two layers — "I think" wraps around "I'd like coffee." The second has three — "He said" wraps around "I think," which wraps around "I would like coffee." Each layer is a clause built from the one inside it.

Classic linguistics party trick, for the curious: "Buffalo buffalo Buffalo buffalo buffalo buffalo Buffalo buffalo." is a grammatically valid sentence — it just reuses buffalo as a city name, an animal, and a verb meaning "to intimidate," all at once.

The four sentence types

Simple S + V. "She completed her literature review."
Compound [S+V], + FANBOYS + [S+V]. "She completed her literature review, and she created her reference list."
Complex [Sub-conj + s+v], S+V. "Although she completed her literature review, she still needed to work on her methods section."
Compound-complex S+V + Co-conj + [S+V + Sub-conj + s+v]. "She completed her literature review, but she still needs to work on her methods section even though she finished her methods course last semester."
One wrinkle worth knowing: a dependent clause can sit before or after the independent clause it's attached to — that changes the comma, not the sentence type.
  • Dependent clause first: comma required — "Although she completed her review, she still needed to work on her methods section."
  • Dependent clause last: usually no comma — "She still needed to work on her methods section although she completed her review."

Coordinating conjunctions — FANBOYS

These join two independent clauses of equal weight (the backbone of a compound sentence):

forandnorbutoryetso

Subordinating conjunctions — by clause type

A subordinator turns a clause into a dependent one. Which subordinator you use depends on the job that dependent clause is doing.

In an adjective clause (relative pronouns)

  • who, whom, whose (of which)
  • that, which
  • where (in which, at which)
  • when (on which)
  • why (for which)

In a noun clause

  • that, if, whether (or not)
  • what, whatever, which, whichever
  • who, whom, whose, whoever, whomever
  • where, wherever, when, whenever, why
  • how, however, how soon/long/many/much/often

Concept cards

Do you understand the rule? Tap a card to check.

CONCEPT · 18 CARDS
ConceptWhat's the language hierarchy, largest to smallest?
AnswerSentences → Clauses → Phrases → Words.
ConceptWhat must every sentence contain to express a complete thought?
AnswerAt least one independent clause — a subject and a verb that can stand alone.
ConceptWhat's the formula for a simple sentence?
AnswerS + V. — one independent clause, no dependent clauses.
ConceptWhat's the formula for a compound sentence?
Answer[S+V], + FANBOYS + [S+V]. — two or more independent clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction.
ConceptWhat's the formula for a complex sentence?
AnswerAn independent clause + a dependent clause joined by a subordinating conjunction. The dependent clause can come before or after.
ConceptWhat's the formula for a compound-complex sentence?
AnswerAt least two independent clauses AND at least one dependent clause — using both a coordinating and a subordinating conjunction.
ConceptWhat does FANBOYS stand for?
Answerfor, and, nor, but, or, yet, so — the seven coordinating conjunctions.
ConceptWhat kind of clause do who / whom / which / that introduce?
AnswerAn adjective (relative) clause — it describes a noun.
ConceptName two subordinators that can introduce a noun clause.
Answerthat, if, whether — or any wh-word (what, who, where, when, why, how...) used as subject or object.
ConceptWhich subordinators show concession — an unexpected result?
Answeralthough, even though, though.
ConceptWhich subordinators show direct contrast?
Answerwhile, whereas.
ConceptGive three subordinators that show reason.
Answerbecause, since, as (also: for the reason that).
Concept"While" can mean two different things. What are they?
AnswerTime — "at the same time as" ("She studied while he cooked"). Contrast — "whereas" ("She loves tea, while he prefers coffee").
ConceptIf the dependent clause comes BEFORE the independent clause, what punctuation do you need?
AnswerA comma right after the dependent clause.
ConceptIf the dependent clause comes AFTER the independent clause, do you usually need a comma?
AnswerNo — usually no comma is needed.
Concept"He said I think I would like coffee." How many clauses are nested inside each other?
AnswerThree — "He said" wraps around "I think," which wraps around "I would like coffee."
ConceptWhat do prepositions build, and where does that sit in the hierarchy?
AnswerPrepositional phrases (preposition + noun/pronoun) — at the "phrase" level, one step below clauses.
ConceptTrue or false: a striking image with no subject or verb can be a complete sentence.
AnswerFalse. However meaningful it looks, without a subject and a verb it can't function as a sentence.

Exam practice cards

Real question formats — identify, fill, and fix.

EXAM · 12 CARDS
Identify the type"She completed her literature review."
AnswerSimple — one independent clause (S+V).
Identify the type"She completed her literature review, and she created her reference list."
AnswerCompound — two independent clauses joined by "and."
Identify the type"Although she completed her literature review, she still needed to work on her methods section."
AnswerComplex — dependent clause ("Although...") + one independent clause.
Identify the type"She completed her review, but she still needs to work on her methods section even though she finished her methods course last semester."
AnswerCompound-complex — two independent clauses joined by "but," plus a dependent clause with "even though."
Fill the gap"She wanted to finish early, ___ her computer crashed."
Answerbut — a FANBOYS conjunction showing an unexpected result.
Fill the gap — concession"___ it was raining, the match continued."
AnswerAlthough / Even though / Though — any one signals concession.
Fill the gap — reason"She left early ___ she felt sick."
Answerbecause / since / as — all show reason.
Spot the problem"Because the exam was difficult."
AnswerSentence fragment — "Because" makes it dependent; it needs an independent clause, e.g. "...many students struggled."
Fill the gap — purpose"She studied hard ___ she could pass the exam."
Answerso that — shows purpose.
True or false"Whereas" and "while" can both signal direct contrast between two clauses.
AnswerTrue.
Identify the clause"The book, which I borrowed yesterday, is due tomorrow."
AnswerAdjective clause — "which I borrowed yesterday" describes "the book."
Identify the clause"I don't know whether she will come."
AnswerNoun clause — "whether she will come" is the object of "know."
